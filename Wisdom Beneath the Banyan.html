Verse Source:
Bhagavad Gita, Chapter XV, Verse 1

English Translation:
“They speak of an imperishable ashvattha tree with its roots above and branches below. Its leaves are the Vedic hymns, and one who knows this tree knows the Vedas.”

Transliteration:
ūrdhvamūlamadhaḥśākham aśvatthaṁ prāhur avyayam |
chhandāṁsi yasya parṇāni yastaṁ veda sa vedavit ||

Grammatical Analysis:

  • “ūrdhvamūlam”, “adhaḥśākham”, “aśvattham”, and “avyayam” are in the nominative case, indicating the subject of the sentence.
  • “prāhuḥ” is in the 3rd person plural, indicating “they speak” or “they say”.
  • “chhandāṁsi” and “parṇāni” are in the nominative plural case.
  • “yasya” is in the genitive case, showing possession.
  • “yastam” and “sa” are in the nominative case, indicating the subject of the latter part of the verse.
  • “veda” is the verb in the 3rd person singular, present tense.

Phonetic Features:

  • Sandhi rules apply in the verse, for instance, “aśvatthaṁ” + “prāhur” = “aśvatthaṁ prāhur”.

Synonyms and Alternate Meanings:

  • अश्वत्थ (aśvattha) can also refer to the sacred fig tree, which is a symbol of impermanence in Indian philosophy.

Cultural or Philosophical Significance:

The Banyan tree, or Ashvattha, symbolizes the eternal and imperishable nature of the soul and the universe in Hindu philosophy. The roots above signify the eternal Brahman or the ultimate source, and the branches below represent the material world. The leaves as Vedic hymns signify the teachings and knowledge that guide one towards moksha or liberation.

Story Depicting the Essence of the Verse:


Once in a village, a wise old man was teaching the village children under a large banyan tree. He wanted to impart the wisdom of life and existence to these young minds.


Pointing towards the tree, he said, "This banyan tree is like the universe. Its roots, which are above, represent the ultimate source or Brahman, and its branches which spread out below signify our material existence.


Each leaf of this tree is like the Vedic hymns, which contain the knowledge and wisdom to understand this universe and our purpose in it. Those who can understand the significance of this tree can understand the Vedas and the ultimate truth of life."

And so, under the shade of the banyan tree, the children learned not just about the world around them, but also about the deeper truths of existence.
